02 · Critical flawDiagnosis Missed opportunity
The diagnosis

The Critical Flaw

The problem is not the science. The technology to replace fossil carbon with biological carbon exists, works, and is deployable now. The flaw is in how the system around it is governed.

Flaw 01

Innovation stalls and taxpayers' money is wasted.

Public funding supports early-stage bio-based innovation, but policy barriers prevent scale-up - leaving ideas stuck in the lab and failing to deliver economic or environmental impact for the very taxpayers who funded them.

Flaw 02

Investment is discouraged.

Higher compliance costs and policy uncertainty push investors towards fossil-based incumbents or overseas markets. Bio-based materials can face hundreds of pounds per tonne in additional compliance costs.

Flaw 03

Industrial inertia continues.

Businesses remain locked into fossil carbon because current policy signals favour the status quo. The system was built around fossil inputs, and nothing in the regulatory framework encourages a shift.

Flaw 04

UK competitiveness and net zero goals are undermined.

Companies relocate abroad. Skilled jobs are lost. Over 4,000 skilled jobs and up to £500 million in annual economic value have already been foregone. Continued fossil reliance locks in avoidable CO2 emissions.

03 · ProblemThe carbon question A building block of modern life
The carbon question

The Problem

Carbon is a building block of modern life. Today, most of it comes from fossil fuels.

Almost everything in a modern supply chain has carbon in it. Plastics, solvents, paints, textiles, fertilisers, pharmaceutical precursors, the adhesives holding your phone together. For more than a century we have sourced that carbon, overwhelmingly, from oil, gas and coal. It was cheap, abundant and concentrated, and the industrial system was built around it.

These foundations are now moving. Fossil carbon is becoming more expensive and less secure, both politically and environmentally. But the cost of fossil fuels is almost always framed as a question of energy - heating, transport, power generation. There is another critical dimension. Fossil fuels are also the primary raw material for thousands of everyday products, from packaging and textiles to pharmaceuticals and adhesives. That material dependency is less visible, but no less urgent.

Bio-based carbon, shorthand for carbon sourced from plants, microbes and organic waste rather than from fossil fuels, offers a replacement at the molecular level. It is not a future technology. Commercial-scale plants already produce bio-based plastics, bio-based solvents, bio-based jet fuel, bio-based animal feed. The UK has world-leading research in the underlying chemistry and some of the best university departments working on it.

The science is proven. The technology works. The bottleneck is policy, not capability.

What it does not have is a coherent market. A company considering a UK bio-refinery faces a different set of rules for its feedstock, its process, its product and its end-of-life waste. Each rule was written for a different problem. Together, they make deployment slower and more expensive here than in France, the Netherlands or the US. A clear lead department, a funded strategy, and a single regulatory front door would not change the science. They would change the economics.

Policy barriers

01 · No single lead department
Bio-based carbon policy currently threads through DESNZ, Defra, DSIT and DBT, with inputs from HMT. No one has a clear brief to set strategy. The result is four consultations and no industrial plan.
02 · Feedstock rules written for waste, not material supply
Agricultural residues, food-processing co-products and municipal organics are classed for disposal, not for manufacturing inputs. A bio-refinery sourcing them inherits a waste-regulation burden that a fossil-refinery does not face.
03 · No public procurement signal
Government contracts do not yet ask for bio-based specifications where they could. Public procurement is one of the fastest, cheapest ways to build an early market. It is currently unused.
04 · Capital allowances favour incumbents
Tax treatment of new bio-refinery capital does not reflect the front-loaded investment profile. Relative to a retrofit of a fossil-asset, a new bio-asset carries a higher effective tax rate in its early years.

04 · SolutionFive-point roadmap From brief to built
The solution

A roadmap for a UK BioRevolution

Five moves the Government can make in one Parliament. Together they turn a research strength into an industrial sector.

1

Name the lead department

One minister, one brief, one door for industry.

2

Publish the strategy

A ten-year bio-based carbon plan, with targets and funding.

3

Fix the feedstock rules

Residues and co-products as inputs, not as waste.

4

Use public procurement

Bio-based specifications in government contracts by default.

5

Back first-of-a-kind plants

Co-funded capital for the first commercial bio-refineries here.

05 · Case studiesIn practice Packaging · Regulation
In practice

Case studies

Two worked examples of what a coherent bio-based carbon policy unlocks in practice. Click to expand.

Case 01 · Packaging

From bio-based bottle to high-street shelf in 18 months.

+

A UK bio-refinery holding a patent on a drop-in replacement for a common fossil-derived polymer signed an offtake deal with a major household-goods brand in late 2024. Under current rules, the product still cannot be sold into the UK grocery supply chain at scale without additional compliance work that would not apply if the feedstock came from a fossil source.

The petition's Point 3 (feedstock rules) and Point 4 (public procurement) would remove both of those drags. The estimated impact is an 18-month faster path from first pilot batch to a supermarket shelf, at a cost to the exchequer of zero.

Case 02 · Regulation

Same product, two regulators, one unfinished market.

+

A bio-based solvent, chemically identical to its fossil-derived counterpart, is handled by one set of rules when sold as a pharmaceutical excipient and by another when sold as an industrial degreaser. The same molecule, the same factory, two regulatory pathways. Small producers give up on one of them. The market ends up split.
