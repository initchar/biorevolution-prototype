**The Critical Flaw**

Bio-based solutions are often treated less favourably than fossil-based alternatives across policy, regulation, and taxation. This creates significant barriers for sustainable alternatives and increases compliance costs for bio-based chemicals and materials — sometimes by several hundreds of pounds per tonne.

This leads to four major consequences:

1. **Innovation stalls and taxpayers’ money is wasted** – Public funding supports early-stage bio-based innovation, but policy barriers prevent scale-up, leaving ideas stuck in the lab and failing to deliver economic or environmental impact for the very taxpayers who have funded this. 

2. **Investment is discouraged** – Higher compliance costs and policy uncertainty push investors toward fossil-based incumbents or overseas markets. 

3. **Industrial inertia continues** – Businesses remain locked into fossil carbon because current policy signals favour the status quo. 

4. **UK competitiveness and net zero goals are undermined** – Companies relocate abroad, skilled jobs are lost and continued fossil reliance locks in avoidable CO2 emissions. 

**The Problem**

Manufactured chemicals are essential to modern life — from the food we eat and clothes we wear to batteries, packaging, and medicines. These products all depend on carbon as a fundamental building block, and today that carbon is sourced predominantly from fossil fuels.

Fossil fuels are often associated only with energy, but they are also the primary raw material for thousands of everyday products. When fossil carbon is extracted and incorporated into materials, it introduces new CO₂ into the atmosphere, creating a direct link between everyday products and climate change. 

Chemicals manufacturing is responsible for nearly 10% of global greenhouse gas emissions, highlighting the climate impact of continued reliance on fossil-based carbon.

The urgency extends beyond climate. Continued dependence on fossil carbon exposes the UK to major economic risks. A single price shock — triggered by Russia’s invasion of Ukraine in 2022 — cost the UK £183 billion over four years, roughly equivalent to the entire cost of the net zero transition to 2050\. Ongoing geopolitical instability continues to demonstrate how reliance on fossil resources threatens both environmental goals and economic stability.

To meet climate targets and strengthen resilience, the UK must transition from fossil carbon to renewable carbon already above ground — including biomass — through the adoption of bio-based solutions.

**What are bio-based solutions?**

Bio-based solutions are products, materials, chemicals, or processes made wholly or partly from biological resources instead of fossil fuels like oil, coal, or gas.

These biological resources can include:

* Plants (e.g., crops, forestry residues)   
* Agricultural or food waste    
* Microorganisms (e.g., bacteria, yeast, algae)    
* Marine biomass (e.g., seaweed)  

In simple terms, bio-based solutions use renewable carbon from nature rather than fossil carbon from underground.

Common examples

* Bio-based plastics made from corn starch or sugarcane   
* Biodegradable packaging from plant fibres   
* Bio-based chemicals produced using fermentation instead of petrochemicals   
* Sustainable textiles from cellulose or agricultural waste    
* Bio-based fuels such as bioethanol or biodiesel   
* Biobased ingredients in cosmetics and detergents 

Bio-based solutions can:

* Significantly reduce CO₂ emissions   
* Strengthen UK supply chain resilience   
* Create thousands of skilled jobs   
* Support rural and industrial growth   
* Help deliver Net Zero 

Despite strong ambitions, the UK is failing to convert investment in bio-based innovation into real economic impact. Over the past decade, nearly £0.5 billion of taxpayer funding has supported early-stage bio-based technologies. Yet less than 10% of projects progress beyond early development, leaving promising innovations stuck in the lab rather than reaching the market.

The problem is not a lack of innovation — it is a lack of coherent policy. The UK funds bio-based innovation with one hand while regulatory frameworks work against it with the other. Businesses face a “valley of death” at scale-up, where support drops away and policy barriers increase.

A major obstacle is the inconsistent treatment of bio-based solutions across policy, regulation, and taxation. Current frameworks often fail to recognise their renewable nature. At the same time, policies focus heavily on recyclability metrics rather than full lifecycle environmental performance, penalising many more innovative and environmentally sound bio-based solutions.

* **DESNZ’s Biomass Strategy** favours traditional energy uses of biomass, such as combustion, over higher-value material applications, limiting availability of sustainable feedstocks for bio-based chemicals and materials.   
* **Defra’s recycling policies (EPR)** place higher compliance costs on many bio-based materials because they are assessed primarily on recyclability, even where they deliver lower lifecycle emissions or improved environmental outcomes.   
* **HMT’s Plastic Packaging Tax** treats bio-based plastics the same as virgin fossil plastics, while fossil plastics containing recycled content can be exempt, reducing incentives to switch to renewable carbon.   
* **Government’s elongated regulatory system** means it is often twice as costly and takes significantly longer for bio-based chemicals to gain market approval compared to fossil-based incumbents, discouraging innovation and SME growth.   
* **DESNZ’s Emissions Trading Scheme** does not consistently recognise the carbon benefits of bio-based materials, meaning fossil-derived products can face similar or lower carbon costs despite higher lifecycle emissions. The result is stark: bio-based materials can face hundreds of pounds per tonne in additional compliance costs, widening the price gap with fossil-based alternatives and discouraging investment.

These conflicting policy signals undermine wider government strategies promoting advanced materials, engineering biology, and biomanufacturing. The consequences are already visible — over 4,000 skilled jobs lost and £300–500 million in annual economic value for the UK foregone. Without urgent corrective action, further company closures, stalled innovation, and relocation of investment overseas are likely — putting UK competitiveness, resilience, and climate progress at serious risk.

**The Solution**

**A roadmap for a UK BioRevolution**

* Establish clear, consistent definitions of bio-based solutions across all relevant policies and regulations. 

* Align carbon accounting, lifecycle assessment, and emissions reporting to reflect the benefits of bio-based solutions. 

* Ensure taxation and incentives support — rather than disadvantage — bio-based products, including within recycling and packaging taxes and emissions trading schemes. 

* Encourage investment in sustainable bio-based materials, fuels, and chemicals to strengthen UK industrial competitiveness. 

* Provide policy clarity for innovators, investors, and industry to remove barriers to scaling bio-based solutions.

**Case Study: UK Packaging Policy Blocking Bio-Based Solutions** 

**The challenge**  
The UK generates around 12 million tonnes of packaging waste annually, yet only 53.7% of plastic packaging was recycled in 2024\. Incinerating plastic packaging produces an estimated 3 million tonnes of fossil CO₂ each year, comparable to the carbon footprint of major UK cities such as Manchester or Glasgow. Recovery rates remain particularly poor for films and small plastics (around 7%), and even with mandatory film collection expected in 2027, performance is projected to remain low.

This highlights a clear need for bio-based alternatives that reduce reliance on fossil plastics and improve circularity.

**Government ambition**  
Multiple UK strategies recognise the importance of bio-based materials for growth and sustainability, including:

* UK Modern Industrial Strategy 

* Engineering Biology Vision 

* National Materials Innovation Strategy 

* UK Plastics Pact 

* Delivering a Net Zero National Health Service 

The UK has also invested heavily, with over £450 million in public R\&D funding for bio-based chemicals and materials between 2018 and 2024\. The sector could grow into a £4.2 billion domestic industry, supporting 35,000 skilled jobs.

**Innovation is happening — but policy is holding it back**  
Public funding has supported projects such as seaweed-based packaging, plant-protein coatings, biochar foams, and compostable materials. However, bio-based packaging currently costs up to 2.5 times more than fossil alternatives due to early-stage supply chains and scale.

Instead of helping close this gap, current policy frameworks widen it.

**Where policy creates barriers**   
Several major policy reforms — including:

* Plastic Packaging Tax (PPT) 

* Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) 

* UK Emissions Trading Scheme 

* Simpler Recycling 

— assess materials primarily on recyclability, rather than full lifecycle environmental performance.

This creates three key barriers:

1. Bio-based materials penalised as “hard to recycle”  
   Many innovative bio-based packaging solutions are classified as difficult to recycle, even when they reduce fossil carbon or offer compostability. This results in higher compliance costs. 

2. Tax system favours fossil plastics  
   The Plastic Packaging Tax treats bio-based plastics the same as virgin fossil plastics, while fossil plastics containing recycled content can be exempt. 

3. Higher compliance costs  
   Combined policy impacts can increase costs for bio-based materials by hundreds of pounds per tonne, widening the price gap with fossil plastics. 

**The contradiction**

These policy signals conflict directly with government ambitions to grow:

* engineering biology 

* advanced materials 

* biomanufacturing 

* domestic green industries 

Despite strong strategic support, the operational regulatory framework discourages adoption.

**Real-world impact**  
Independent analysis suggests that failure to support the domestic bio-based sector has already contributed to:

* Loss of over 4,000 skilled jobs 

* £300–500 million in annual economic value lost 

* Companies relocating investment overseas 

**Why this matters**  
The UK is at a critical decision point. Without policy alignment:

* Fossil plastics remain the cheapest option 

* Public investment in innovation is undermined 

* Bio-based alternatives struggle to scale 

* Net zero and circular economy goals are harder to achieve 

**Key takeaway**

UK policy currently rewards recyclability metrics over lifecycle carbon performance, unintentionally penalising bio-based packaging and slowing the transition away from fossil plastics.

This case illustrates how misaligned policy — not lack of innovation — is blocking bio-based solutions.

**Case Study: Outdated Regulation Blocking Bio-Based Innovation in the UK** 

**The ambition**  
The UK Modern Industrial Strategy sets out a vision for the UK to become one of the world’s top three hubs for technology-driven innovation by 2035, including the ambition to create the UK’s first trillion-dollar tech company.

At the heart of this vision is Engineering Biology — a sector focused on designing and manufacturing fuels, chemicals, materials, food, and medicines using biological systems. The global bioeconomy is expected to be worth over £30 trillion by 2050, and the UK Government has committed £2 billion over the next decade to support its development.

---

**The policy gap**  
Despite strong strategic backing, the UK’s regulatory framework for bio-based chemicals and materials remains outdated and fragmented. Current systems were largely designed for fossil-derived products and do not reflect the characteristics of modern bio-based innovation.

This creates:

* Unclear approval pathways 

* Longer regulatory timelines 

* Higher data requirements 

* Increased costs for innovative SMEs 

Countries such as the United States, EU member states, and Singapore are actively updating regulatory systems to accelerate bio-based innovation — putting the UK at risk of falling behind. 

---

**What businesses actually need**  
For bio-based companies to scale, three things are essential:

1. Predictable timelines and decision rules 

2. Proportionate, risk-based data requirements 

3. Market incentives that reward sustainability 

Independent research from the Bio-based and Biodegradable Industries Association shows that current UK frameworks fall short across all three.

**Where regulation creates barriers**   
Regulatory complexity is now the second-largest barrier to commercialisation of bio-based innovation in the UK.

In the cosmetics ingredients sector, the difference is stark:

* Fossil-based ingredient approval: 7 stages 

* Bio-based ingredient approval: 11 stages 

Approval timelines:

* Fossil-based ingredient: \~50 weeks 

* Bio-based ingredient: \~72 weeks (45% longer) 

Approval costs:

* Fossil-based ingredient: \~£372,000 

* Bio-based ingredient: \~£815,000 (more than double) 

This means companies developing more sustainable bio-based ingredients face higher costs, longer delays, and greater uncertainty than fossil-based competitors.

**Real-world economic impact**   
For just three UK-based SMEs, regulatory delays are estimated to result in:

* £35.7 million in lost annual Gross Value Added (GVA) 

* 473 potential jobs unrealised 

With hundreds of bio-based SMEs across the UK, the total economic impact is likely in the tens of billions annually.

**The contradiction**   
While national strategy promotes engineering biology and sustainable manufacturing, regulatory frameworks:

* Are designed around fossil inputs 

* Penalise novel bio-based innovation 

* Increase costs for sustainable alternatives 

* Discourage SME growth and investment 

This creates a systemic mismatch between policy ambition and regulatory reality.

**Why this matters**  
If regulatory barriers remain:

* Investment will shift overseas 

* UK SMEs will struggle to scale  

* Public funding for innovation will be undermined 

* The UK risks losing leadership in engineering biology 

**Key takeaway**

The UK’s regulatory system unintentionally favours fossil-based chemicals by making bio-based alternatives slower and more expensive to approve, directly undermining national ambitions for engineering biology and sustainable growth.

This case study demonstrates how outdated regulation — not lack of innovation — is blocking bio-based solutions in the UK.
